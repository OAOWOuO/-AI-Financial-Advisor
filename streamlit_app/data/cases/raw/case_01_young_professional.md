# Young Professional with Student Loan Burden
**Case ID:** case_01_young_professional | **Life Stage:** Early Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Sarah Chen, age 27, is a software analyst at a mid-size tech firm in Austin, TX. Single, no dependents, renting a one-bedroom apartment. She graduated four years ago with $52,000 in federal student loans and has been making minimum payments while building her career. Sarah has a stable job but feels financially stagnant — her net worth is essentially flat despite two years of salary increases.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Gross salary: $68,000/year ($5,667/month gross; ~$4,200 net after taxes and benefits)
- Monthly take-home breakdown: rent $1,400, food $400, transport $320, subscriptions/entertainment $200, student loan payment $580, miscellaneous $300
- Estimated monthly surplus: ~$1,000 before any savings

### Assets
- Checking/savings: $3,200 (covers ~1.4 months of expenses)
- 401k balance: $0 (has not enrolled despite employer offering 4% match)
- Brokerage/investments: $0

### Liabilities & Debt
- Federal student loans: $48,000 remaining at blended 6.5% interest rate
- Credit card: $0 (paid in full monthly — positive habit)

### Insurance Coverage
- Health insurance: employer-sponsored (good coverage)
- Life insurance: none (no dependents — appropriate for now)
- Disability insurance: employer group short-term only
- Renters insurance: none

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **No retirement contributions** — Not enrolled in 401k, leaving 4% employer match unclaimed (= $2,720/year of free money left on the table every year)
2. **Insufficient emergency fund** — $3,200 covers only 1.4 months of expenses vs. the 6-month benchmark ($15,600 needed)
3. **Student loan opportunity cost** — At 6.5%, these loans represent a guaranteed 6.5% cost of capital; the question is whether to prioritize payoff vs. investing
4. **No renters insurance** — Replacing personal belongings in a loss event could wipe out the emergency fund entirely
5. **No long-term disability coverage** — Group coverage typically limited to 60% of salary and ends at job termination

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
Sarah is in the "financial foundation" phase of planning. The single most impactful action available to her is 401k enrollment to capture the employer match — this represents a 100% immediate return on the first 4% of salary contributed. The $48,000 in student loans at 6.5% is above the risk-free rate, so accelerated payoff is mathematically competitive with investing in a taxable account, but the 401k match dominates both.

The emergency fund gap is significant. At only 1.4 months, any unexpected expense (job loss, medical bill, car repair) would force Sarah to use debt. Building this to 6 months before aggressively paying off student loans is prudent sequencing. Once the EF is funded, a "debt avalanche" approach targeting the highest-rate loans first maximizes interest savings.

The absence of renters insurance is a quick, inexpensive fix that eliminates a significant tail risk for under $200/year. Long-term disability coverage should be explored through professional associations or the voluntary benefits open enrollment window.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Immediately enroll in 401k** at minimum 4% to capture full employer match — adds $2,720/yr at zero cost to Sarah
2. **Build emergency fund to $10,000** (first milestone) before aggressive debt payoff — automate $400/month transfer
3. **Refinance student loans** if credit score supports a lower rate (target < 5.5%), but avoid variable-rate products
4. **Purchase renters insurance** ($15–18/month) to protect against loss scenarios
5. **Explore voluntary LTD** through employer — group rates are significantly lower than individual policies
6. Once EF reaches $10K, redirect $400/month to accelerated student loan payoff using debt avalanche

## Follow-Up Questions
- What is your current credit score? (Determines refinancing viability)
- Does your employer offer a Roth 401k option? (Tax diversification consideration given current low bracket)
- Do you have any irregular income (bonuses, RSUs, side work) that could accelerate EF building?
- Are your student loans federal or a mix of federal/private? (Federal loans have income-driven repayment and forgiveness options)
- What is your career trajectory? Are you likely to change employers in the next 2 years? (Affects 401k vesting)

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
After implementing these recommendations over 18 months, Sarah's financial position improved substantially: emergency fund reached $10,000, 401k balance grew to $8,400 (including match), and student loan principal dropped by $12,000 through a combination of refinancing (from 6.5% to 5.2%) and accelerated payments. Net worth turned positive at +$14,000. The key lesson: **capturing employer match is the highest-return financial decision available to most early-career employees and should never be deferred.**
