# Dual-Income Family with College Funding Pressure
**Case ID:** case_02_family_college_planning | **Life Stage:** Family Formation | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Jordan (38) and Sofia Martinez (36), married 10 years, live in suburban Chicago with two children: Mateo (8) and Isabella (6). Jordan is a marketing director ($92,000) and Sofia is a registered nurse ($56,000), for a combined gross income of $148,000. They own their home with $285,000 in equity and feel financially comfortable but increasingly anxious about college costs and retirement adequacy.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Combined gross: $148,000/year; net ~$9,200/month after taxes, healthcare, and 401k contributions
- Monthly expenses: mortgage $2,400, food $900, childcare/activities $1,200, transportation $700, insurance $380, utilities/subscriptions $350, entertainment $300
- Monthly surplus: ~$2,970 — but most of it is currently uncommitted

### Assets
- 529 College Savings Plans: $14,000 combined (both accounts) — significantly underfunded
- 401k (Jordan): $88,000 at 6% contribution, 3% employer match
- 401k (Sofia): $54,000 at 4% contribution, no employer match (hospital system)
- Emergency fund: $28,000 (adequate — approximately 4.5 months)
- Home equity: $285,000

### Liabilities & Debt
- Mortgage: $340,000 remaining at 3.25% fixed (15 years left)
- Car loans: $18,000 combined at 4.9%
- Credit cards: $0 balance

### Insurance Coverage
- Health: employer-sponsored for both
- Life: Jordan $250,000 group term; Sofia $180,000 group term — both inadequate given income/mortgage
- Disability: group short-term through employers only
- No umbrella policy

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **College funding gap** — At current 529 balance and contributions, projected college funding covers less than 25% of estimated 4-year costs for each child ($180,000+ in 2030 and 2032 dollars)
2. **Life insurance underinsured** — Combined household needs approximately $1.8M–$2.1M in coverage (10–12x income + mortgage payoff) vs. $430,000 currently
3. **Sofia's retirement savings rate low** — Contributing only 4% with no employer match yields an estimated 12% overall savings rate vs. 15% recommended minimum
4. **No long-term disability insurance** — Replacing either income for 2–5 years would be catastrophic without LTD coverage
5. **Competing financial goals** — Monthly surplus is unallocated; without a prioritized framework, it accumulates in low-yield savings

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
The Martinez family is in the classic "accumulation and protection" phase with multiple competing priorities. The college vs. retirement dilemma is real: federal financial aid does not consider retirement accounts (401k, IRA), so maximizing retirement contributions is generally prioritized over 529 contributions. However, with Mateo entering college in 10 years and Isabella in 12, the window is closing.

A Roth IRA ladder strategy is compelling here: contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn penalty-free for education expenses, giving the Roth double duty as both a retirement vehicle and a college funding backup. This is especially valuable for Sofia, who has no employer match and is in a lower tax bracket.

The life insurance gap is the most urgent risk. If Jordan were to die with only $250,000 in coverage, Sofia would face an immediate income shortfall plus a $340,000 mortgage on a $56,000 salary with two children. A 20-year term policy for Jordan of $1.2M–$1.5M can be obtained for approximately $80–110/month at his age and health status.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Immediate: increase Jordan's life insurance** to $1.5M 20-year term (~$95/month) and Sofia's to $750K 15-year term
2. **Redirect $600/month to 529 plans** ($300 per child) — increases college coverage from 25% to ~55% of projected costs
3. **Sofia: open and fund Roth IRA** to $7,000/year — serves dual retirement + college funding purpose
4. **Explore umbrella liability policy** ($1–5M coverage for ~$25–40/month) given combined income and home equity
5. **Jordan: increase 401k to 8%** — can be funded by slight reduction in current unallocated surplus
6. **Annual financial review** to rebalance college vs. retirement allocation as tuition estimates firm up

## Follow-Up Questions
- What type of colleges are the children likely to target? (In-state public vs. private significantly changes projections)
- Does Sofia's hospital system offer a 403(b) or 457 plan in addition to the 401k?
- Have you considered a 529 superfunding strategy using 5-year gift tax averaging?
- What is your mortgage payoff strategy — do you plan to sell the home before retirement?
- Would Jordan's employer benefits allow supplemental group life insurance at lower rates than individual market?

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Over three years, the Martinezes implemented the life insurance recommendation immediately and started the Roth IRA for Sofia. College funding reached $68,000 combined by year 3. The key lesson: **in family formation cases, life insurance is the first priority, and the Roth IRA serves as a powerful hybrid vehicle for households caught between competing retirement and education goals.**
