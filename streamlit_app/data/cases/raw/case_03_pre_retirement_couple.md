# Pre-Retirement Couple: 10-Year Countdown
**Case ID:** case_03_pre_retirement_couple | **Life Stage:** Pre-Retirement | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Robert Johnson (55) is a senior engineer at a defense contractor, and Linda Johnson (52) is a high school principal. Combined income of $215,000. They have two adult children and a paid-off home. Targeting retirement at 65 (Robert) and 62 (Linda). Their biggest concern: whether their savings will sustain 30+ years of retirement given the "sequence-of-returns risk" window they're entering.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Robert: $135,000/year; Linda: $80,000/year; combined $215,000
- Monthly expenses: $6,200 (housing maintenance, food, travel, utilities, healthcare)
- Monthly surplus: ~$9,000 before retirement contributions

### Assets
- 401k/IRA combined: $820,000
- Employer stock (Robert's company): $180,000 (35% of portfolio — concentration risk)
- Home equity: $520,000 (paid off)
- Brokerage: $85,000
- Total net worth: ~$1,605,000

### Liabilities & Debt
- Mortgage: $0
- Car loans: $0
- Credit cards: $0

### Insurance Coverage
- Health: employer-sponsored (transitions to Medicare at 65 for Robert)
- Life: $400,000 combined (adequate given mortgage-free status; adult children)
- LTC insurance: NONE — major gap
- Disability: group coverage through both employers

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **Retirement gap under conservative scenario** — Conservative projection (5% return, 3.5% SWR) shows a $340,000 shortfall; Balanced scenario shows near-parity
2. **No long-term care insurance** — At 55/52, premiums are still manageable ($3,500–$5,500/year per person); waiting 5 more years increases cost significantly and risks insurability
3. **Concentrated employer stock** — Robert's $180,000 in company stock represents 22% of total portfolio; diversification needed before retiring into that position
4. **Healthcare gap** — Linda retires at 62 but Medicare doesn't start until 65; 3 years of private coverage needed (~$18,000–$24,000/year)
5. **Roth conversion opportunity** — Pre-retirement high-income years + anticipated lower income in early retirement create a Roth conversion window before RMDs begin at 73

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
The Johnsons are in an excellent financial position but face the common "accumulation-to-distribution" transition challenges. The most time-sensitive issue is LTC insurance: the probability of needing care increases sharply after 65, and premiums at age 55 are roughly 2–3x lower than at 65. A hybrid life-LTC policy can provide death benefit if LTC is not needed.

Employer stock concentration is a frequently overlooked risk. Robert's entire career income plus 22% of his retirement portfolio are correlated with one company's performance. A systematic diversification plan — selling $30,000–$45,000 in employer stock per year and reinvesting in index funds — reduces this risk while managing capital gains tax exposure.

The Roth conversion strategy is compelling: in early retirement years (ages 65–72, before Social Security optimization and before RMDs), the Johnsons will likely be in the 22–24% bracket rather than the 32% bracket they're in today. Converting $50,000–$100,000 per year during this window reduces future RMDs and provides tax diversification.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Purchase LTC insurance now** — Both Robert and Linda, hybrid life/LTC policies preferred; cost ~$4,500/year each
2. **Diversify employer stock** over 5 years: sell $36,000/year in Robert's company stock and invest in total market index funds
3. **Maximize catch-up contributions** — Both are 50+, can contribute $30,500 to 401k and $8,000 to IRA each year
4. **Budget for Linda's healthcare gap** — Earmark $60,000–$75,000 in accessible (non-retirement) funds for bridge coverage ages 62–65
5. **Begin Roth conversion planning** — Model the conversion window starting at Robert's retirement (age 65) through RMD onset
6. **Social Security optimization** — Robert delays to age 70 (32% premium over FRA); Linda files at 65–67 based on projection

## Follow-Up Questions
- Does Robert have a defined benefit pension or deferred compensation through his employer?
- What is Linda's teacher pension situation — does she have a state pension and what is the vesting status?
- What are your travel and lifestyle goals in early retirement vs. late retirement? (Affects spending curve modeling)
- Have you considered geographic arbitrage — would you move to a lower-cost state/tax jurisdiction in retirement?
- What is Robert's employer stock cost basis? (Determines NUA strategy viability)

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Within 2 years of implementing the plan, the Johnsons had purchased LTC insurance, reduced employer stock to 12% of portfolio, and increased annual retirement contributions by $24,000. Under the balanced scenario, the retirement gap closed to near zero. Key lesson: **the 10-year pre-retirement window is the most impactful planning period, and LTC insurance and stock concentration risk are the two issues most commonly deferred too long.**
