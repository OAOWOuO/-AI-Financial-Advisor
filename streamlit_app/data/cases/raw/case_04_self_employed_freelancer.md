# Self-Employed Freelancer: Irregular Income Planning
**Case ID:** case_04_self_employed_freelancer | **Life Stage:** Mid-Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Michael Patel, 35, is a freelance cloud architect working primarily for three recurring clients. Single, no dependents. His income has grown significantly since leaving his corporate employer three years ago but varies substantially — averaging $95,000 but ranging from $60,000 (slow months) to $140,000 (heavy project months). He received an IRS underpayment notice last year and is concerned about both tax efficiency and retirement savings without an employer match.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Gross revenue: ~$95,000/year average (highly variable; some months $4,000, others $14,000)
- Self-employment tax: ~$13,400/year (15.3% on first $160,200)
- Income tax: ~$18,000/year (after SE deduction)
- Monthly living expenses: ~$4,800 (rent $1,600, food $500, transport $350, health insurance $480, business expenses $600, misc $1,270)
- After-tax, after-expense monthly average: ~$3,100 — but timing mismatch causes cash flow problems

### Assets
- Checking + business savings: $28,000 (5-6 months at average income, but 3-4 months at high-expense periods)
- SEP-IRA: $18,000 (only 2 years of contributions)
- Equipment/tools: $12,000 (depreciable business assets)

### Liabilities & Debt
- No consumer debt
- IRS penalty (underpayment): $3,200 outstanding

### Insurance Coverage
- Health insurance: ACA marketplace silver plan ($480/month; decent coverage)
- No disability insurance — MAJOR gap
- No life insurance (no dependents; appropriate)
- Business liability: none

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **Quarterly estimated taxes underpaid** — IRS penalty indicates systematic underpayment; no tax payment calendar in place
2. **Emergency fund insufficient for self-employed** — Industry best practice for freelancers is 9–12 months of expenses; Michael has ~5–6 months at average, and income volatility makes this inadequate
3. **SEP-IRA severely underfunded** — At $18,000 balance after 3 years with income of $95,000+, the contribution rate is far below the 25% of net self-employment income maximum ($23,250 limit)
4. **No disability insurance** — Self-employed individuals have NO employer-provided disability benefits; losing 6 months of income to an injury or illness could be catastrophic
5. **Irregular cash flow creates decision paralysis** — High-income months create temptation to overspend; low-income months create anxiety

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
Michael's situation is common among high-earning freelancers who face the "benefits vacuum" of self-employment. The absence of automatic payroll withholding and employer-provided benefits requires a structured system that mimics the institutional discipline of corporate employment.

The solution to the tax problem is a dedicated "tax withholding" savings account: every time a client payment arrives, Michael should immediately transfer 30–35% to a separate account designated for quarterly estimated taxes and the SE tax. This mechanical separation prevents the behavioral error of spending income that is not truly "his."

The SEP-IRA is one of the most powerful tools available. At $95,000 net income, Michael can contribute up to ~$23,000 in 2024, reducing his taxable income by the same amount. A Solo 401k is even more flexible — it allows both employee contributions ($23,000) and employer profit-sharing contributions (25% of net SE income), potentially sheltering $46,000+ annually.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Open a Solo 401k** (not SEP-IRA) — allows larger contributions and Roth option; target $30,000/year
2. **Implement 30% tax reserve account** — every client payment triggers automatic 30% transfer to a dedicated tax savings account; pay quarterly from this account
3. **Build emergency fund to 9 months** (~$43,000) before other investing; target $18,000 current gap over 18 months
4. **Purchase individual disability insurance** — group unavailable; individual policy targeting 60% of income (~$4,750/month benefit) costs approximately $120–180/month
5. **Implement income smoothing** — pay yourself a consistent "salary" of $5,500/month from business account; excess accumulates as buffer

## Follow-Up Questions
- What is your typical client contract structure — project-based or retainer? (Affects income predictability)
- Have you considered incorporating (S-Corp election)? At $95K+ income, S-Corp can reduce SE tax by $4,000–$8,000/year
- Do any of your clients offer W-2 employment or are all engagements 1099?
- What is your health insurance plan's out-of-pocket maximum and is an HSA eligible?
- Do you have business liability exposure from your consulting work?

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Michael implemented the tax reserve account immediately and avoided underpayment penalties the following year. He opened a Solo 401k and contributed $27,000 in year one, building a $45,000 balance by year two. An individual disability insurance policy was secured. Key lesson: **self-employed clients need structured financial systems that replace the institutional infrastructure of corporate employment — automatic withholding, forced savings, and explicit insurance planning are non-negotiable.**
