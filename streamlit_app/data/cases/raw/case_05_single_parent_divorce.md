# Divorced Single Parent: Rebuilding Financial Security
**Case ID:** case_05_single_parent_divorce | **Life Stage:** Mid-Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Jennifer Williams, 42, divorced two years ago after a 12-year marriage. She has one daughter, Emma, age 11. Jennifer works as a project manager at a healthcare company ($82,000/year) and receives $12,000/year in spousal support (which ends in 3 years when Emma turns 14). She owns a home jointly awarded in the divorce, retaining $95,000 in equity with a $1,850/month mortgage.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Salary: $82,000/year; spousal support: $12,000/year; total $94,000/year
- Effective income in 3 years: $82,000 (loses $12,000/year spousal support)
- Monthly net: ~$5,800 after taxes
- Monthly expenses: mortgage $1,850, food $600, transport $450, Emma's activities $400, healthcare $280, misc $700
- Monthly surplus: ~$1,520 (shrinks to $500 when spousal support ends)

### Assets
- Checking/savings: $22,000 (emergency fund — adequate)
- 401k: $68,000
- Home equity: $95,000
- No investment accounts

### Liabilities & Debt
- Mortgage: $235,000 at 4.75% (28 years remaining)
- Car loan: $18,400 at 5.9% (4 years remaining)
- Credit card debt: $8,200 (carried balance — high-priority payoff)

### Insurance Coverage
- Health: employer-sponsored (adequate)
- Life insurance: $50,000 employer group term — CRITICALLY INADEQUATE (Emma needs 15+ years of income protection)
- Disability: group short-term only (no LTD)
- No umbrella policy
- Will: exists but still names ex-husband as beneficiary and executor — URGENT UPDATE NEEDED

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **Life insurance critical gap** — At $50,000 coverage vs. a need of $1.1M–$1.4M (15x income for Emma's support), Jennifer is massively underinsured; she is Emma's primary provider
2. **Spousal support cliff** — In 3 years, monthly income drops by $1,000; current spending plans not adjusted for this
3. **Estate documents outdated** — Will and beneficiary designations still name ex-husband; this is both a legal and emotional risk
4. **No college savings** — Emma turns 18 in 7 years; no 529 plan exists; at current 0% savings, Emma faces full loan burden
5. **High-rate credit card debt** — $8,200 at approximately 22% APR while emergency fund earns 4–5%; guaranteed negative-return debt

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
Jennifer faces the "single-income household with dependent" challenge — there is no financial backstop if she becomes disabled or dies. This creates a protection-first planning framework: insurance and estate planning take absolute priority over wealth building.

The life insurance need calculation for Jennifer: 15 years until Emma is independent, $82,000 income replacement (present value ~$900,000), plus $235,000 mortgage payoff, plus college funding buffer ($150,000), less existing assets ($90,000) = approximately $1.2M needed. A 20-year level term policy at her age and health status costs approximately $65–90/month for $1M in coverage — affordable given her surplus.

The credit card debt should be eliminated within 12 months before beginning college savings. A debt avalanche approach (minimum on mortgage/car, extra to credit card) frees up $684/month once the card is paid off, which can be redirected to 529 contributions.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Immediate priority: purchase $1.2M 20-year term life insurance** (~$75/month) — this is Emma's financial safety net
2. **Update all legal documents within 60 days** — new will, change beneficiaries on 401k and life insurance, assign guardianship explicitly
3. **Eliminate credit card debt** in 12 months using $1,200/month extra payments (use surplus + suspend discretionary)
4. **After credit card payoff: open 529 plan** and contribute $300/month; apply for AOTC tax credit when Emma starts college
5. **Pre-plan for spousal support expiration** — begin lifestyle adjustment 6 months before it ends; consider increasing salary via certification/promotion push
6. **Explore long-term disability insurance** — individually purchased; $60,000/year benefit; estimated $150–180/month

## Follow-Up Questions
- Does Emma's father carry any life insurance or child support obligations that affect Jennifer's income-replacement need?
- What is Emma's college ambition — in-state, out-of-state, community college? (Significantly changes 529 target)
- Does Jennifer's employer offer supplemental life insurance through group rates? (Lower cost than individual)
- Has Jennifer consulted a divorce financial analyst regarding the home equity decision — is keeping the house the optimal choice financially?
- What is Jennifer's credit score? (Affects mortgage refinance viability and life insurance pricing)

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Jennifer purchased $1.2M in term life insurance within 30 days and updated her will and beneficiary designations within 60 days. The credit card was paid off in 14 months. A 529 plan was opened with $250/month automated contributions. Key lesson: **single-parent cases require a protection-first sequencing where insurance and estate documents precede any wealth-building strategy — the financial exposure of being a single provider with a dependent cannot be overstated.**
