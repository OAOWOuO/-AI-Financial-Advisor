# High-Income DINK Couple: Accelerated Wealth Building
**Case ID:** case_06_dual_income_no_kids | **Life Stage:** Early Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
David Kim (33) is a senior software engineer at a publicly traded company earning $175,000 base + $60,000 in annual RSU vesting. Sophia Kim (31) is a data scientist at a biotech firm earning $115,000. Combined W-2 income: $290,000 + $60,000 RSUs. Married 3 years, no children by choice, renting in Seattle. Both work remotely and are financially sophisticated but lack a structured optimization plan.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Combined W-2: $290,000; RSU income: ~$60,000/year (taxed at vesting)
- Effective monthly take-home (after 401k, taxes): ~$14,500
- Monthly expenses: rent $3,200, food $800, transport $400, travel $1,000, subscriptions/discretionary $800
- Monthly surplus: ~$8,300 — largely sitting in savings account earning 4.5%

### Assets
- Cash/savings: $145,000 (excess cash — opportunity cost)
- 401k (David): $85,000 at 6% contribution; employer match 4%
- 401k (Sophia): $60,000 at 6% contribution; employer match 3%
- Employer stock (David's RSUs, unvested): $185,000
- Employer stock (vested, held): $85,000 (concentrated position — 32% of investable assets)
- No taxable brokerage account

### Liabilities & Debt
- No debt of any kind
- No estate plan, no wills

### Insurance Coverage
- Health: both employer-sponsored (excellent coverage with HSA eligibility)
- Life: David $200,000 employer term; Sophia $150,000 — low need given dual income, no dependents
- Disability: group only

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **Concentrated employer stock** — $85,000 vested RSUs represents 32% of investable portfolio in one stock; classic single-security concentration risk
2. **Mega backdoor Roth opportunity unused** — At $290,000 combined W-2, both are above traditional Roth IRA income limits; backdoor Roth IRA ($14,000 total) not implemented
3. **No taxable brokerage account** — $145,000+ in low-yield savings when they have fully funded emergency fund; this represents significant opportunity cost
4. **No estate plan** — No wills, no powers of attorney, no beneficiary review; assets would pass by intestacy law if one partner died
5. **HSA triple tax advantage not used** — Neither is contributing to HSA despite HSA-eligible health plans
6. **401k contribution rate suboptimal** — At $290,000 income, both could afford to maximize ($23,000 each + $7,500 if 50+) but both contribute only 6%

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
The Kims are in the enviable position of having a large financial surplus but lacking a systematic deployment framework. Their excess cash earning 4.5% is actually a reasonable emergency fund reserve plus opportunity cost — the real issue is that the $145,000 minus a 6-month emergency fund (~$38,000) should be in the market.

The concentrated stock position is the highest-risk item. David's $85,000 in company stock means his human capital (salary/career), unvested RSUs, and 32% of his investment portfolio are all correlated with one company's performance. A rule-of-thumb: no single stock should exceed 10% of investable assets. A systematic selling program (selling as RSUs vest, using covered calls on the position) can diversify over 3–4 years.

The backdoor Roth IRA is a no-brainer: Congress has not closed this strategy as of 2024, and at their income level, tax diversification (traditional pre-tax vs. Roth after-tax) is critically valuable over a 30+ year horizon. Both partners should execute backdoor Roth contributions annually.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Execute backdoor Roth IRA** for both ($7,000 each = $14,000 total); set up in January each year
2. **Maximize both 401ks to $23,000 each** — current 6% contributions leave significant tax-deferred space on the table
3. **Open HSA accounts** and maximize contributions ($4,150/person in 2024); invest in index funds rather than holding cash in the HSA
4. **Systematic RSU diversification** — sell all RSUs at vesting (or shortly after); reinvest in total market index fund via taxable brokerage account
5. **Open taxable brokerage account** — invest $6,000/month from surplus after maximizing all tax-advantaged accounts
6. **Create basic estate plan** — wills, healthcare proxies, POAs, updated beneficiary designations

## Follow-Up Questions
- Does David's 401k plan offer after-tax contributions with in-plan Roth conversion (mega backdoor Roth)?
- Are you planning to purchase a home in the next 3–5 years? (Affects liquid vs. invested allocation)
- Have you modeled the AMT implications of exercising any non-qualified stock options or ISOs?
- What is your target retirement age — early retirement (FIRE) or conventional 65?
- Do you hold the employer stock in a taxable account (affects NUA strategy viability)?

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Within one year, the Kims maximized both 401ks, executed backdoor Roth contributions, opened HSA accounts, and sold 60% of the concentrated stock position. Taxable brokerage account grew to $180,000. Key lesson: **high-income dual-earner households with no children often have large untapped tax-advantaged contribution space and concentrated stock risk — a structured contribution and diversification plan can materially improve expected retirement wealth and reduce portfolio volatility.**
