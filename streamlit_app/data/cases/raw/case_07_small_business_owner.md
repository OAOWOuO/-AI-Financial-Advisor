# Small Business Owner: Succession and Retirement Gap
**Case ID:** case_07_small_business_owner | **Life Stage:** Mid-Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Carlos Hernandez, 48, owns and operates an HVAC installation and service company with 14 employees in Phoenix, AZ. His wife Elena (45) manages the office part-time for a salary of $32,000. Business generates $185,000 in owner compensation (W-2 equivalent) plus approximately $45,000 in annual distributions. Carlos has a 50/50 business partner, James, and they founded the business 12 years ago. Business valuation: approximately $620,000 (3.5x EBITDA). No formal succession plan, no buy-sell agreement, expired key-man insurance.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Owner compensation + distributions: $230,000/year combined household
- Elena's salary: $32,000 (included above or separate — need clarification)
- Monthly household expenses: $8,200 (mortgage, 3 kids' activities, healthcare, vehicles)
- Monthly surplus: ~$5,600 available for savings/business reinvestment

### Assets
- Traditional IRA: $85,000 (only individual retirement savings; no company plan)
- Home equity: $180,000 (mortgage $420,000 remaining)
- Business equity (50% stake): $310,000 (illiquid; subject to partner agreement)
- Cash savings: $65,000 (emergency fund + business buffer)

### Liabilities & Debt
- Mortgage: $420,000 at 6.25%
- Business line of credit: $80,000 drawn ($200,000 limit)
- Equipment financing: $45,000 at 7.2%

### Insurance Coverage
- Health: small group plan through business
- Life: $250,000 term (personal); no key-man insurance (expired 2 years ago)
- No disability insurance on business owner
- No buy-sell agreement with James

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **No qualified retirement plan for business** — $85,000 in IRA after 12 years of $230,000+ income is severely underfunded; Solo 401k or SEP-IRA could shelter $50,000+/year
2. **Business concentration risk** — 60% of Carlos's net worth ($310,000 business equity) is illiquid, in one business, in one industry, in one geographic market
3. **No buy-sell agreement** — If James dies, becomes disabled, or wants to exit, there is no mechanism to manage ownership transfer; Carlos could end up with James's heirs as partners
4. **Key-man insurance expired** — The bank that issued the business line of credit required key-man insurance; it lapsed; business could face credit line reduction and operational disruption if Carlos is incapacitated
5. **No succession plan** — What happens to the business at Carlos's retirement, disability, or death? The $620,000 of value could be destroyed without a transition plan

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
Carlos represents the "illiquid concentration" problem common to small business owners: the majority of his wealth is tied up in an illiquid, undiversified asset. His retirement security depends entirely on either selling the business or generating sufficient income from it — and neither is guaranteed.

The retirement plan gap is immediately actionable. A Solo 401k allows Carlos to contribute $23,000 as employee plus 25% of his compensation as employer profit sharing — potentially $58,750 in 2024. For context: if he had done this for the past 10 years, his retirement balance would be approximately $720,000 rather than $85,000. The tax savings alone at his income level are roughly $20,000/year.

The buy-sell agreement is the most urgent legal issue. A cross-purchase buy-sell agreement, funded by life insurance on each partner, ensures that if one partner dies, the surviving partner can purchase the deceased's share from the estate at a predetermined price. Without this, Carlos could face an unworkable partnership with James's heirs.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Establish Solo 401k immediately** — contribute maximum employee deferral ($23,000 + $7,500 age 50 catch-up = $30,500) plus profit-sharing; target $50,000/year total contribution
2. **Draft buy-sell agreement within 90 days** — engage business attorney; cross-purchase structure funded by life insurance on both Carlos and James
3. **Reinstate key-man life insurance** on both partners ($300,000–$500,000 each) to protect business credit facilities and operations
4. **Disability buyout insurance** — if Carlos becomes disabled, the buy-sell triggers at disability onset; this requires a separate disability buyout policy (~$400–600/month)
5. **Business succession planning** — engage business broker and M&A advisor to assess options: third-party sale, family succession, ESOP; 5-year timeline before Carlos's target retirement at 55
6. **Accelerate personal investment diversification** — use distributions above living expenses to fund taxable brokerage account to reduce dependence on business exit

## Follow-Up Questions
- What is the business succession preference: sell to a third party, pass to a family member, or partner buyout?
- Has the business been formally valued recently by a certified business appraiser (not just Carlos's estimate)?
- Does James have a succession plan or exit timeline that might conflict with Carlos's?
- What is the condition of the business line of credit covenants — is the key-man insurance a covenant requirement?
- Does Carlos have a non-compete agreement from his corporate employment that would restrict a future sale?

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Carlos established a Solo 401k within 45 days, contributing $47,000 in year one. A buy-sell agreement was drafted and signed, funded by $500,000 life policies on each partner. Key-man insurance was reinstated. Key lesson: **small business owners systematically underfund personal retirement while over-concentrating in illiquid business equity — the combination of a qualified retirement plan and a buy-sell agreement with proper insurance funding is the minimum viable risk mitigation framework.**
