# Widowed Retiree: Decumulation and Estate Planning
**Case ID:** case_08_widowed_retiree | **Life Stage:** Retirement | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Margaret Thompson, 68, was widowed 18 months ago. Her husband of 40 years left a well-organized estate. She has three adult children and six grandchildren. Margaret retired at 66 from teaching; her husband was a retired physician. She lives in Seattle, WA. Her primary concerns are ensuring her assets last her lifetime, minimizing taxes on RMDs, and creating a meaningful legacy for her grandchildren and the local food bank she has supported for 20 years.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Social Security: $22,400/year (stepped up to survivor benefit after husband's death)
- State teacher pension: $28,200/year (COLA-adjusted, joint and survivor — continues at full amount)
- Required Minimum Distributions: ~$24,000/year (based on $890,000 combined IRA balance)
- Total annual income: ~$74,600
- Monthly expenses: $4,200 (housing, healthcare, travel to see family, charitable giving $400/month)
- Monthly surplus: ~$2,000 — effectively funding savings she doesn't need

### Assets
- Inherited IRA (husband's): $580,000 (subject to 10-year withdrawal rule for surviving spouse — actually she rolled into own IRA)
- Own IRA/403b: $310,000
- Taxable brokerage: $185,000
- Primary home: $430,000 (paid off)
- Total estate: approximately $1.84M

### Liabilities & Debt
- None

### Insurance Coverage
- Medicare Parts A, B, D (supplement plan)
- No long-term care insurance — SIGNIFICANT GAP given $1.84M estate
- Life insurance: $150,000 universal life (from decades ago, low cash value)

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **RMD tax drag** — At age 73, RMDs on $890,000 are approximately $36,000/year growing annually; combined with pension and SS, Margaret is in the 22–24% bracket and being pushed toward 32% bracket; she doesn't need the income
2. **Washington State estate tax exposure** — WA estate tax applies above $2.193M; at current trajectory, Margaret's estate will exceed this threshold within 3–5 years of investment growth; effective WA estate tax rate on amounts above threshold: 10–20%
3. **No LTC insurance** — At age 68, individual LTC insurance is difficult to obtain and expensive; a long nursing home stay ($120,000–$180,000/year in Seattle) could rapidly deplete her estate
4. **Charitable giving inefficient** — Current $4,800/year in cash donations provides a 37% deduction value if she itemizes, but Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) strategy from IRA provides 100% effective deduction at her income level
5. **No formal estate plan post-spouse death** — Will needs to be rewritten reflecting new family circumstances and legacy goals

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
Margaret's situation requires a shift from accumulation to intentional distribution. Her pension, Social Security, and RMDs provide more income than she spends — she is effectively a forced accumulator. The planning challenge is managing RMDs to minimize taxes, address LTC risk, and efficiently transfer wealth to grandchildren and charity.

The Qualified Charitable Distribution strategy is the single highest-impact immediate action: Margaret can direct up to $105,000/year from her IRA directly to qualified charities, satisfying RMD requirements without the distribution appearing as income. At her marginal rate, this is equivalent to an additional $10,000–$22,000 in effective charitable deduction vs. cash giving.

For estate tax, a Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT) is no longer applicable (she's widowed), but a charitable remainder trust (CRT) can serve multiple goals: (1) convert appreciated assets to an income stream, (2) create a charitable deduction, and (3) ultimately transfer the remainder to the food bank. Alternately, annual gifts to grandchildren ($18,000/year per person exclusion = $108,000/year tax-free to six grandchildren) can systematically reduce the estate below the WA threshold.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Implement QCD strategy** — donate $18,000–$24,000/year directly from IRA to food bank (satisfies RMD, excludes from income)
2. **Annual exclusion gifting to grandchildren** — $18,000 per grandchild × 6 = $108,000/year estate reduction; establish 529 plans for college-age grandchildren
3. **Establish Charitable Remainder Trust** for the food bank legacy goal; CRT provides income stream to Margaret and passes remainder to charity
4. **Consult WA estate tax attorney** — model estate projection under current growth assumptions; consider gifting strategies to reduce below $2.193M threshold
5. **LTC self-insurance planning** — at $1.84M estate, consider earmarking $250,000 as a dedicated LTC reserve in a segregated, conservative investment allocation
6. **Update estate documents** — new will, healthcare directive (critical given widowhood), updated beneficiaries, trustee designations

## Follow-Up Questions
- Have the beneficiary designations on the IRAs been updated to reflect her children (not her late husband)?
- Does Margaret want to leave the home to her children or sell and downsize?
- What are her LTC preferences — stay at home with aides, assisted living, or memory care if needed?
- Is the food bank a DAF-eligible organization, and would a Donor Advised Fund create more flexibility?
- Does Margaret have professional advisors (CPA, attorney) who are already coordinating? (WA state estate planning is specialized)

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Margaret implemented QCD giving within 3 months, reducing her taxable income by $22,000 and satisfying RMDs. Annual gifting to grandchildren reduced estate by $108,000/year. A charitable remainder trust was established for the food bank. Key lesson: **widowed retirees with income-generating assets often face a "forced accumulation" problem where they receive more income than they need — strategic distribution through QCDs, gifting, and trusts can simultaneously minimize taxes, reduce estate tax exposure, and fulfill legacy goals.**
