# Military Family: TSP Optimization and Deployment Planning
**Case ID:** case_09_military_family | **Life Stage:** Early Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Tyler Anderson (29, Army Captain, O-3E) and Emily Anderson (27, civilian HR coordinator) live at Fort Campbell, KY. They have two children, ages 4 and 2. Tyler is currently in his third year on a six-month deployment to the Middle East. Under the Blended Retirement System (BRS), Tyler is vested after 2 years of service and earns a multiplier-based pension. The family moves every 2–3 years due to PCS (Permanent Change of Station) orders, making homebuying complex.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Tyler: $78,000 base pay + $22,000 BAH (non-taxable); deployment adds combat zone tax exclusion
- Emily: $38,000 civilian salary
- Total gross (tax-adjusted equivalent): ~$138,000/year
- Monthly net: ~$8,400 after taxes and benefits deductions
- Monthly expenses: $4,200 (BAH covers housing fully on base)
- Monthly surplus: ~$4,200 — significantly higher during deployment due to lower expenses

### Assets
- TSP balance: $28,000 (contributing 5% — receives 5% government match, but base match is 1% automatic + 4% match)
- Savings: $18,000
- Emily's 401k: $12,000 (5% contribution, 3% match)
- SGLI: $400,000 (maximum coverage; $28/month — excellent value)
- No home (renting on base — appropriate given PCS frequency)

### Liabilities & Debt
- Car loan: $14,200 at 5.5%
- No other debt

### Insurance Coverage
- TRICARE: exceptional comprehensive health coverage for family (near zero cost)
- SGLI $400,000 maximum (Tyler); Emily has employer life insurance $50,000
- No personal disability (VA disability rating from prior service injury would provide coverage if service-connected)

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **TSP severely under-contributed** — At 5%, Tyler captures full match but leaves significant retirement savings on the table; maximum is $23,000 ($30,500 with catch-up); at current rate, TSP will only reach ~$400,000 by retirement age 20 (age 40 for early separation)
2. **Combat zone Roth TSP contribution opportunity missed** — During deployment, Tyler's pay is largely exempt from federal income tax; this is the ideal window to maximize Roth TSP contributions (contributions are after-tax but distributions are tax-free)
3. **BRS vs. legacy retirement decision** — Tyler joined after Jan 2018 so he's under BRS automatically; he should model whether staying until 20 years (pension = 40% of base pay) vs. separating earlier and maximizing TSP is optimal
4. **PCS housing strategy** — Moving every 2–3 years and buying a home each time creates transaction costs (realtor fees, closing costs) that destroy equity; on-base housing or renting remains optimal
5. **Emily's income continuity risk** — As a civilian spouse following military assignments, Emily faces career disruption risk and may need to rebuild her career with each PCS move

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
Military families have access to financial benefits that are often underutilized. TRICARE saves approximately $15,000–$25,000/year compared to civilian health insurance. The SGLI provides $400,000 in life insurance for $28/month — an extraordinary value. TSP offers institutional index funds at some of the lowest expense ratios available (~0.06% in the Lifecycle Funds).

The deployment Roth TSP strategy is the most powerful immediate opportunity. During a combat deployment, Tyler's base pay is excluded from federal taxes. If he redirects his entire TSP contribution to Roth TSP during deployment, he's effectively contributing post-tax dollars that will never be taxed again — and in a year where the tax cost is zero. This is a once-in-career tax arbitrage opportunity.

The BRS pension modeling depends heavily on Tyler's career intentions. A 20-year military career with 40% pension is worth $624,000 in present value (at age 40 with 40+ year payment horizon). However, the foregone TSP contributions and civilian career income must be weighed against this.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Maximize Roth TSP during deployment** — Redirect 100% of TSP contributions to Roth TSP while pay is tax-exempt in combat zone ($23,000 limit)
2. **After deployment: continue at 15% Roth TSP contribution rate** — at $78,000 base pay, $11,700/year still qualifies for full government match
3. **Emily: maximize employer 401k to capture full match** — increase from 5% to at least 6% to capture 3% employer match fully
4. **VA loan planning for next PCS** — if Tyler's next assignment is 3+ years, a VA loan purchase may make sense; model rent-buy NPV with VA loan benefit
5. **BRS survivor benefit plan (SBP)** — if Tyler reaches 20 years, SBP election (covering Emily at 55% of pension) is a critical retirement decision; model vs. private life insurance
6. **Emily's career continuity** — remote work or federal civilian employment opportunities at each new base assignment

## Follow-Up Questions
- How many years has Tyler served and what is his anticipated career endpoint?
- Is Tyler's deployment income above or below the annual TSP limit (determining how much Roth TSP can be contributed)?
- Does Emily have a federal civilian employment preference from the Military Spouse Preference program?
- What is Tyler's VA disability rating if any from prior deployments?
- Does Tyler receive any foreign language pay, hazardous duty pay, or special operations bonuses that affect the planning?

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Tyler maximized Roth TSP contributions during deployment, adding $18,500 in tax-free Roth contributions. Emily increased her 401k to capture full match. TSP balance grew to $68,000 in two years. Key lesson: **military families have extraordinary financial benefits (TRICARE, SGLI, TSP matching, VA loan, combat zone tax exclusion) that are systematically underutilized — a deployment window represents a rare opportunity to front-load Roth retirement savings at near-zero tax cost.**
