# First-Time Homebuyer: Down Payment vs Financial Security
**Case ID:** case_10_first_time_homebuyer | **Life Stage:** Early Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
Miguel Garcia, 31, is a marketing manager at a mid-size company in Denver, CO. Single, no dependents. He has been renting a one-bedroom for $1,800/month and has been saving aggressively for a down payment. He has $42,000 earmarked for home purchase and $12,000 in a separate emergency fund. Target home price: $380,000. His concern is whether he has enough saved and whether buying now is the right financial decision given elevated interest rates (~7.0% in early 2024).

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- Gross salary: $88,000/year (~$5,850/month net)
- Monthly expenses (renting): $1,800 rent, $450 food, $320 transport, $180 utilities/subscriptions, $250 misc = $3,000/month
- Monthly surplus while renting: ~$2,850

### Assets
- Down payment savings (HYSA): $42,000
- Emergency fund: $12,000 (4.3 months of current expenses)
- 401k: $38,000 (contributing 6%, getting 4% employer match)
- No other investments

### Liabilities & Debt
- Car loan: $15,000 at 5.9% (3 years remaining, $430/month payment)
- Student loans: $0 (paid off 2 years ago)
- Credit cards: $0 balance

### Insurance Coverage
- Health: employer-sponsored
- Life: none (no dependents — appropriate)
- Renters insurance: $18/month (appropriate)

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **Down payment adequacy** — $42,000 is only 11% of $380,000; this triggers PMI, typically 0.5–1.0% of loan balance annually ($1,600–$3,200/year); 20% down ($76,000) is needed to avoid PMI
2. **Emergency fund erosion** — If Miguel uses $42,000 for down payment plus $12,000–$15,000 in closing costs, emergency fund could be fully depleted at the time of highest financial exposure (new homeowner)
3. **Housing cost ratio at boundary** — Principal+interest on $338,000 at 7.0% = $2,249/month; plus taxes ($400), insurance ($150), HOA ($0): PITI = $2,799. As a percentage of $7,333 gross monthly income: 38.2% — above the 28% benchmark
4. **DTI constraint** — Including car loan payment ($430): total monthly debt = $3,229. DTI = $3,229 / $7,333 = 44% — approaching lender maximums (typically 43–45%)
5. **Rate environment** — At 7.0%, monthly P+I on a $338,000 loan = $2,249 vs. $1,487 at 3.5% (the 2020-2021 environment); total interest cost over 30 years is dramatically different

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
Miguel faces the classic first-time buyer dilemma: he has saved diligently but not quite enough to buy without PMI while also maintaining financial security. The question is not just "can he afford the mortgage" but "should he buy now vs. waiting 18–24 months to save more."

Financial analysis of the decision:
- **Buy now at 11% down**: PMI of ~$2,200/year until equity reaches 20%; total cost over 3 years vs. renting: approximately $8,000 more than renting (accounting for appreciation, principal paydown, and tax benefits in a moderate appreciation scenario)
- **Wait 18 months and save 20% down**: saves ~$2,200/year in PMI, avoids depleting emergency fund, but pays $32,400 in rent (opportunity cost if home appreciates)

The Colorado Housing Finance Authority (CHFA) and other first-time buyer programs offer down payment assistance, reduced MI rates, and below-market interest rates for qualifying buyers — these can significantly change the math.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Investigate Colorado first-time buyer programs** — CHFA and others may provide grants or below-market rate mortgages that change the affordability calculation
2. **If buying: target 15% down minimum** ($57,000) — requires saving an additional $15,000; continue at $2,500/month savings for 6 more months before closing
3. **Maintain $15,000 emergency fund at closing** — do not let closing costs drain emergency fund below 3 months; adjust purchase price or use gift funds if needed
4. **Pay off car loan aggressively** — $15,000 at 5.9%; paying off reduces DTI by 5.9 percentage points (to 38%), significantly improving lender risk profile
5. **Consider 15-year vs. 30-year mortgage** — Miguel's income supports a 15-year payment; over the life of the loan, the interest savings are $120,000+, but monthly payment is higher by ~$700
6. **Rate buydown analysis** — with current 7.0% rates, a 1-point buydown costs ~$3,380 and reduces rate to 6.0%; break-even is 18–24 months

## Follow-Up Questions
- Is Miguel eligible for Colorado CHFA or other first-time buyer programs? (Income and purchase price limits apply)
- Does his employer offer any home purchase assistance benefits?
- What is Miguel's credit score? (720+ qualifies for best conventional rates; 740+ optimal)
- Is there flexibility on the purchase price? ($340,000–$350,000 homes would meaningfully change the DTI calculation)
- Has Miguel considered a 2–1 rate buydown strategy given current rate environment?

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
Miguel waited 8 months, saved $20,000 more (reaching $62,000 in down payment savings), paid off the car loan, and purchased at $365,000 with 17% down. With mortgage insurance, the housing cost ratio came to 33%. Key lesson: **first-time buyers should model the full cost of PMI, the DTI impact of existing debt, and the financial security implications of emergency fund depletion — a 6–12 month wait to reach a stronger financial position often dominates the cost of continued renting.**
