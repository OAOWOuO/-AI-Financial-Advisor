# Inheritance Windfall: Integrating a Sudden Wealth Event
**Case ID:** case_12_inheritance_windfall | **Life Stage:** Mid-Career | **Year:** 2024

## Client Background
William Davis, 44, is a civil engineer at a municipal government agency in Charlotte, NC. Married to Patricia (42, elementary school teacher). Two children: ages 14 and 11. William unexpectedly inherited $395,000 from his aunt 8 months ago, consisting of $180,000 cash/CDs, a $135,000 inherited IRA (traditional), and a $80,000 taxable brokerage account with highly appreciated positions. The inheritance has been sitting largely untouched in a money market account while William and Patricia experience "windfall paralysis" — unable to decide how to deploy the assets.

## Financial Situation

### Income & Cash Flow
- William: $112,000 (civil engineer, public sector pension eligible)
- Patricia: $58,000 (teacher, state pension participant)
- Combined gross: $170,000 ($118,000 after taxes and retirement contributions)
- Monthly net: ~$9,833
- Monthly expenses: $3,400 mortgage, $1,100 food, $600 transport, $800 children's activities/education, $420 utilities, $480 misc = $6,800
- Monthly surplus: ~$3,033

### Assets
- Inherited cash (HYSA/money market): $168,000 (from original $180,000 less some family expenses)
- Inherited IRA: $135,000 (traditional; subject to 10-year distribution rule for non-spouse beneficiary)
- Inherited taxable brokerage: $80,000 (highly appreciated positions — cost basis ~$18,000)
- Own 401k/retirement: $95,000 (contributing 6%, getting 4% employer match)
- Patricia's state pension: ~10 years of service credit; vested
- 529 plans: $12,000 combined for both children
- Home equity: $148,000 (home value $420,000, mortgage $272,000 at 3.25%)
- Total estimated net worth: ~$706,000

### Liabilities & Debt
- Mortgage: $272,000 at 3.25% (17 years remaining)
- Car loan: $18,500 at 6.1%
- No other debt

### Insurance Coverage
- Health: employer-sponsored (William, family plan)
- Life: William $300,000 term; Patricia $150,000 employer term — likely adequate but should review given higher net worth
- Disability: group short-term + long-term at both employers
- No umbrella liability insurance — significant gap given now-higher net worth

### Windfall Psychology Note
William and Patricia describe feeling "frozen" — afraid of making the wrong decision with "someone else's money." They have not sought professional advice until now, 8 months post-inheritance. This is textbook windfall paralysis, common in sudden wealth events. Immediate decision pressure is low, but the inherited IRA has mandatory distribution requirements beginning year 1.

## Planning Issues Identified
1. **Inherited IRA 10-year rule** — As a non-spouse beneficiary, William must distribute all inherited IRA assets within 10 years of the aunt's death; failure results in a 25% excise tax; no distributions have been taken in year 1 (8 months elapsed); while year 1 distributions are not mandatory, a distribution strategy needs to be modeled now to avoid a large tax spike in years 8-10
2. **Inherited taxable brokerage: step-up in basis opportunity** — The $80,000 taxable brokerage received a step-up in cost basis to fair market value at date of aunt's death; if William sells immediately, capital gains tax is near-zero (only gains since inheritance date, ~8 months); if he holds, future appreciation creates capital gains; the strategic window to rebalance tax-free is NOW
3. **Windfall cash deployment** — $168,000 earning ~5% in HYSA is reasonable short-term but suboptimal long-term; deployment framework needed: emergency fund top-up, debt paydown, 529 funding, investment allocation
4. **529 plan severely underfunded** — $12,000 for two children against estimated $80,000–$150,000 per child for 4-year college; 4 years until oldest starts college; aggressive catch-up needed
5. **No umbrella liability insurance** — At ~$706,000 net worth, underlying auto and homeowners policies ($300K/$500K limits) may be insufficient; a $1–2M umbrella policy adds meaningful protection for ~$200–$400/year
6. **Own retirement underfunded** — $95,000 at age 44 with a $170,000 household income represents significant underfunding; monthly surplus plus inheritance can accelerate retirement savings dramatically
7. **Low-rate mortgage dilemma** — 3.25% mortgage rate creates a philosophical tension: prepay (guaranteed 3.25% return) vs. invest (expected higher long-term return); generally, at 3.25%, market investment dominates mathematically

## Planning Analysis & Advisor Notes
William and Patricia's situation is favorable despite the paralysis. They have time (10-year inherited IRA window), a strong income base, and an inheritance that represents meaningful but not overwhelming wealth. The goal is not to "get rich" with the inheritance but to accelerate financial goals already within reach.

The inherited IRA distribution strategy is the most technically complex issue. At their combined $170,000 income, distributing $13,500–$20,000/year from the inherited IRA over 10 years keeps distributions in the 22% bracket. Bunching distributions into low-income years (if Patricia takes a leave of absence, for example) or distributing heavily in years when they have large deductions can be tax-efficient. If they distribute nothing for 9 years and take the full $135,000 in year 10, the entire amount is taxable at once — potentially at 32–35%.

The step-up in basis on the taxable brokerage is the most time-sensitive opportunity. The highly appreciated positions ($18,000 cost basis) received a new basis at death. Selling now and reinvesting in a diversified, low-cost portfolio is nearly tax-free (only gains since the inheritance date are taxable). Every month that passes allows new unrealized gains to accumulate.

## Key Recommendations
1. **Begin inherited IRA distributions immediately** — Target $13,500–$17,500/year (10% of balance) for 10 years; model tax impact at current combined income; adjust distribution amount in years with higher/lower taxable events
2. **Sell inherited taxable brokerage within 30 days** — Realize the step-up in basis benefit; reinvest proceeds in diversified total-market index fund allocation; consult CPA on any short-term gains since inheritance date
3. **Deploy cash windfall in priority order**:
   - a. Emergency fund: top up to $35,000–$40,000 (4 months of $9,000 gross expenses) — keep in HYSA
   - b. Pay off car loan: $18,500 at 6.1% — guaranteed 6.1% return
   - c. Fund 529 plans: $50,000 split equally ($25,000 each child) — urgency given 4-year college timeline for older child; consider superfunding (5-year gift tax exclusion lump sum)
   - d. Maximize retirement contributions: use $20,000–$30,000 to bridge while maximizing 401k contributions to $23,000 each for 2-3 years
   - e. Taxable brokerage: invest remaining $60,000–$70,000 in low-cost index funds
4. **Do NOT pay down 3.25% mortgage** — At 3.25%, expected long-term investment returns (~7%) make prepayment suboptimal; this may change if rates or circumstances change
5. **Purchase $2M umbrella liability policy** (~$300/year) — increases protection across all underlying policies given new net worth level
6. **Update estate plan** — Wills, beneficiary designations, powers of attorney, healthcare directives; inherited assets create new estate complexity; trust structures may be appropriate at this wealth level

## Follow-Up Questions
- When did William's aunt pass away? (Sets the 10-year inherited IRA clock precisely; if she passed before 2020, different rules may apply)
- Has William worked with a CPA on the inherited IRA RMD schedule or the tax impact of any distributions already taken?
- What is the specific composition of the inherited brokerage — individual stocks, funds, or a managed account? (Affects step-up in basis calculation and diversification approach)
- Does William's public sector employer offer a deferred compensation (457b) plan? (As a civil engineer, he may have access to a 457b, which has no 10% early withdrawal penalty — powerful supplement to 401k)
- Is Patricia's state pension a traditional defined benefit (final average salary formula), or a hybrid? (Affects overall retirement income modeling)

## Outcome & Lessons Learned
William and Patricia sold the inherited taxable brokerage within 45 days (minimal capital gains), established a 10-year inherited IRA distribution schedule ($14,000/year), funded both 529 plans to $25,000 each via superfunding, paid off the car loan, and began maximizing both retirement accounts. Key lesson: **sudden wealth recipients need a structured decision framework to overcome paralysis — prioritizing the most time-sensitive decisions first (inherited IRA schedule, basis step-up window) while systematically deploying cash to pre-existing financial goals transforms a windfall from a source of anxiety into a genuine financial acceleration.**
